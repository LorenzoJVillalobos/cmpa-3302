# Organizing System Analysis: Wikipedia

## Introduction

For this assignment, I chose **Wikipedia** as the public knowledge base to analyze. Wikipedia is probably one of the most widely used digital collections because it covers such a huge range of topics and is easy for people to access. It functions as a public knowledge base made up of articles on people, places, events, entertainment, history, science, and just about everything else. What makes Wikipedia interesting from an organizing systems perspective is that it has to manage a massive amount of information while still keeping it usable for both casual readers and people doing more focused research.

---

## Primary Resources Being Organized

The main resources being organized in Wikipedia are its **articles**. Each article is centered on a specific topic, whether that is a movie, historical event, person, concept, or location. For example, if someone looks up *Spirited Away*, the article acts as the main resource and contains information about the film’s plot, production, themes, and reception. In addition to the articles themselves, Wikipedia also organizes supporting materials like images, citations, info boxes, and categories. Those extra pieces help structure the information and make it easier for users to understand a topic quickly.

---

## Primary Interactions Supported by the System

Wikipedia supports a few major types of interaction, mainly **research, browsing, and fact-checking**. A user can search for a topic directly if they already know what they want, but they can also browse by following links from one article to another. That is one of the biggest reasons Wikipedia works so well. Someone might start by looking up one movie or one historical figure and then end up reading about related people, events, or ideas because the system makes those connections easy to follow. It also supports fact-checking because articles usually include references and citations that point users back to outside sources.

---

## Classification System and Its Effectiveness

Wikipedia uses a mix of **hierarchical classification and networked organization**. It has categories that group pages together, such as “Studio Ghibli films” or “Japanese animated fantasy films,” which creates a kind of hierarchy. At the same time, it is heavily built around hyperlinks, so it does not force users into only one path for finding information. I think this makes it effective for its intended audience because people can either search for exactly what they need or move through related topics more naturally. For a general audience, that balance between structure and flexibility works really well.

---

## How Relationships Between Resources Are Represented

Relationships between resources on Wikipedia are represented mostly through **hyperlinks, categories, info boxes, and “See also” sections**. The hyperlinks inside articles are probably the most important because they connect one topic directly to another. For example, an article about *Spirited Away* will link to Hayao Miyazaki, Studio Ghibli, Japanese animation, and other related topics. Categories at the bottom of the page group articles into larger collections, while “See also” sections point users toward related resources that might be worth exploring. Together, these features help users understand not just a single article, but how that topic fits into a broader network of information.

---

## Conclusion

Overall, Wikipedia is a strong example of a digital organizing system because it combines a huge collection of resources with a structure that still feels usable. Its main resources are articles, but the way it supports searching, browsing, and fact-checking is what really makes it effective. The combination of categories, links, and related sections helps users move through information in a way that feels intuitive instead of rigid. For a knowledge base this large, I think Wikipedia does a good job of balancing organization with accessibility.
